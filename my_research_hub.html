How This Rubric Is Different From BCG's
  • Fundamentals-first, technicals second. BCG's Sunday sessions lead with chart setups; mine leads with business quality and valuation. Chart is field 5 of 8.
  • Financials are numeric, not vibes. Rev growth %, GM %, FCF Y/N — fill in numbers or fail the field.
  • Sizing is tied to conviction. Not equal-weight, not vibes. Field 7 forces a written rationale for why the size matches the confidence.
  • Explicit PASS with reason. BCG's watchlist has no "passed" state — mine does, in research/passed.md, so I don't re-litigate the same ticker every month.
  • Review date is required. Every BUY/WATCH has a next-review-date. Nothing sits stale.
Valuation Mini-Reference — What Each Term Means

These are the numbers Field 4 wants. All the ratios below are answering the same question in different ways: am I paying a lot or a little for what this company produces? No single ratio is right for every stock — use the one that fits the business (earnings-based for profitable companies, sales-based for pre-profit growth, book-based for banks/insurers).

TermStands forFormulaHow to read it
P/E Price-to-Earnings Share price ÷ EPS (earnings per share) What you pay per $1 of annual profit. P/E 20 = paying $20 for $1 of profit. Compare to sector median and 5yr avg.
Fwd P/E Forward P/E Share price ÷ next-12-month expected EPS Better for growth — TTM is backward-looking. If forward P/E < TTM P/E, analysts expect earnings to grow.
TTM P/E Trailing Twelve Months P/E Share price ÷ last 4Q actual EPS Backward-looking. Uses only reported numbers, so no analyst optimism.
PEG P/E to Growth Fwd P/E ÷ EPS growth rate (%) Adjusts P/E for growth. PEG < 1 often "cheap for a grower"; PEG > 2 often "priced for perfection." Don't trust for <10% growth names.
EV/Sales Enterprise Value to Sales (Market cap + debt − cash) ÷ Revenue Used when a company isn't yet profitable. EV/S 10 = paying $10 for $1 of annual sales. Adjusts for debt/cash.
EV/EBITDA EV to EBITDA (earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, amortization) (Market cap + debt − cash) ÷ EBITDA The "operating cash flow" version. Used for capex-heavy businesses (industrials, telecom, energy) where GAAP earnings distort.
P/S Price-to-Sales Share price ÷ Revenue per share Simpler EV/Sales without adjusting for debt. Fine for cash-rich, debt-light companies.
P/B Price-to-Book Share price ÷ Book value per share Mostly used for banks, insurers, and REITs where "book" (net assets) is meaningful. P/B 1.0 = trading at accounting equity.
FCF Yield Free Cash Flow Yield FCF ÷ Market cap × 100 What % of your investment comes back as free cash each year. FCF yield 5% ≈ "P/FCF of 20." Higher = cheaper on a cash-generation basis.
Payout Ratio Dividend Payout Ratio Dividends ÷ Earnings (or FCF) For income names only. >80% is a yellow flag — little cushion if earnings dip, dividend may get cut. >100% = paying div from balance sheet.
Yield-on-Cost YOC Current annual dividend ÷ YOUR entry price Income-name magic: buy at $50 with 3% starting yield → 10yr later div grew to $4 → YOC = 8%. What you actually earn on the money you put in.
Net Debt / EBITDA Leverage ratio (Debt − cash) ÷ EBITDA How many years of cash flow would it take to pay off debt. >3x is high for most; utilities/REITs run 5–7x normally.
Rule of thumb: Aggressive/Growth bucket → prioritize Fwd P/E, PEG, EV/Sales, FCF Yield. Income bucket → prioritize Yield, Payout Ratio, Net Debt/EBITDA, Div growth streak.
Field 5 — What "Distance to Buy" Means

Distance to buy: ___% is how far above your buy line current price sits, expressed as the % drop needed to hit the buy. It tells you how imminent an order fill is — useful for prioritizing which orders to place first with limited cash.

Formula: Distance = ((current − buy line) ÷ current) × 100 Example 1 — NVDA at $180, buy line $160 Distance = ((180 − 160) ÷ 180) × 100 = +11.1% Read as: price needs to drop 11% to hit your buy. Example 2 — CRWV at $77.76, buy line $82 Distance = ((77.76 − 82) ÷ 77.76) × 100 = −5.5% Read as: price is already 5.5% BELOW buy — a limit at $82 fills instantly. (This actually happened on 2026-07-15.) Distance ≈ 0% → order will fill soon; place FIRST if cash-limited Distance +1–5% → close; place next Distance +5–10% → wait a beat Distance +10%+ → far away; GTC and forget until an alert fires

Portfolio-Building Rules

  2. ETF-first for satellite themes. If a quality ETF exists for a theme (SMH, QTUM, METV, SCHD, JEPI), it's the default. Singles are add-ons for conviction, not the base.
  3. Don't buy holdings your ETF already gives you. Skip NVDA/AMD/AVGO/TSM/MU as singles if you own SMH. Skip IBM/HON as singles if you own QTUM.
  4. Size to conviction, not equal-weight. Core ETFs $400–800. Conviction singles $200–400. Speculative satellites $100–200 (max 2% of bucket).
  5. Set the stop after every fill. No naked positions. Stops are Stop-Market GTC unless the ETF is a core hold (SMH, SCHD).
  6. Quarterly rubric re-run on every held. If any of the 8 fields fails on re-run, trim or exit. Log the change.
  7. Cap ~20–25 names total across all 3 buckets. More than that = over-diversified; hard to manage.

Exit Rules
  • Stop hit → exit. Don't average down "just to see."
  • Thesis broken (any of rubric fields 1–4 flips negative on re-run) → exit or trim to a starter.
  • Dividend cut on income name → immediate exit. The whole reason it was there is now gone.
  • ETF replaces the reason for the single (e.g., own SMH and NVDA as separate; on next re-run consolidate into SMH) → trim single, keep ETF.
  • Better idea takes the slot — replacements only within the same bucket to preserve balance.
